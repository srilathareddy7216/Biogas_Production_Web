How Biogas Production Works

Anaerobic digestion converts organic waste into biogas (mainly methane and CO₂) through four key stages.

STAGE 01

Hydrolysis

Complex organic polymers (carbohydrates, proteins, fats) are broken down into simple sugars, amino acids and fatty acids by enzymes.

STAGE 02

Acidogenesis

Acid-forming bacteria convert these simple molecules into volatile fatty acids, alcohols, hydrogen and carbon dioxide.

STAGE 03

Acetogenesis

Volatile fatty acids are further converted into acetic acid, hydrogen and carbon dioxide, preparing material for methane formation.

STAGE 04

Methanogenesis

Methanogenic bacteria convert acetic acid, hydrogen and CO₂ into methane (CH₄) — the main combustible component of biogas.

Safety Guidelines

General precautions when working with biogas systems.

Ventilation

Biogas contains methane and traces of hydrogen sulfide (H₂S), which is toxic and flammable. Ensure proper ventilation around digesters and storage.

Temperature Control

Operating outside 20–60°C slows or halts microbial activity, reducing gas yield and potentially producing unstable, low-quality gas.

pH Monitoring

pH below 6 or above 8.5 inhibits methanogens, can cause souring of the digester, and may produce excess CO₂ instead of usable methane.
